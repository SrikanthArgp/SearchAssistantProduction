An HS256 signature is 256 bits, which base64url-encodes to 43 characters where the last character carries only 4 real bits ‚Äî the other 2 are fixed zero padding. `'A'` (000000) and `'B'` (000001) differ only in that final padding bit. Whenever the actual signature's trailing base64 character already has zero in those low bits, flipping `A`‚Üî`B` didn't change the decoded signature bytes at all ‚Äî decode succeeded, no `JWTError` raised, test failed. Since the signature depends on a random `jti` and the current timestamp, this hit roughly 1 in 4 runs, confirmed by re-running it in isolation (failed consistently in this session's runs, passed in an earlier one).
  - **Fix applied** in `tests/phase3_auth/test_jwt.py`: tampers a character in the *middle* of the payload segment (`payload[len(payload)//2]`) instead of the last character of the token. Any non-trailing base64url character always encodes a full 6 real bits (only the very last character of a segment can land on unused padding bits), and changing a payload byte always changes what the signature was computed over ‚Äî so this is deterministic, not a probabilistic improvement.
